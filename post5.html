How I Price Full‑Stack Projects Without Scaring Clients Away

Pricing is one of the trickiest parts of freelancing. Price too high, and you lose the deal. Price too low, and you burn out. Here’s my framework for setting rates that clients accept — and that still pay me well.

Step 1 – Understand the Value

  • Ask: What’s the client’s goal? If your work will help them earn $50k/year more, charging $5k is reasonable.
  • Avoid hourly rates for big projects — they cap your earning potential.

Step 2 – Use the “Three‑Tier” Method

  • Basic: Minimal features, quick delivery.
  • Standard: Most requested features, balanced timeline.
  • Premium: Full customization, priority support, extra integrations.

Step 3 – Factor in Hidden Costs

  • Communication time, revisions, and post‑launch support.
  • Tools, hosting, and third‑party APIs.

Step 4 – Anchor the Price

  • Start with the premium package first — it makes the others look more affordable.
  • Use round numbers (e.g., $2,500 instead of $2,473).

Example: For a custom booking platform: Basic: $1,200 | Standard: $2,500 | Premium: $4,000

Stop selling hours — sell outcomes. Clients pay for results, not time.
